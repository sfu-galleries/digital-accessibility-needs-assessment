Executive Summary

Purpose: Why we do this work

In 2023, SFU Galleries engaged an external consultant to review their digital landscape and processes. The goal was to identify risks and opportunities for growth and community engagement. This assessment focuses on inclusion and accessibility. This work is ongoing. It will constantly shift as technology develops and as SFU Galleries' audience changes. This is a snapshot of a moment in time to provide insight into barriers present in existing processes. It is not a critique on those who are currently engaged in this work, nor those who created it. This report is written from an outside perspective. It both highlights opportunities for change, and identifies barriers that may not be possible to remove with the resources currently available to SFU Galleries. The knowledge gained from this report is equally important as solving conflicts or crossing items off a list. When we are made aware of barriers, we better see those who are struggling. We can have more informed conversations and moments for inclusion, even when the temptation is to “fix” a problem and check it off a list.

Presentation: A note about language and format

Writing Style

This report uses a social language writing style. SFU Galleries sits at the intersection of the academic world, the fine arts community, and the public. It is best to consider the audience with the most challenges and centre them when building access into policy. While universal access is not possible, it is always the goal. This report aims for a grade 10/11 reading level and 60% on the Flesch Reading Ease to strike a balance between accessible and professional. A grade 9 reading level is the recommended reading level when writing content for the web.

SFU Galleries may feel pressure to communicate in more academic ways due to its connection to the University and the Fine Arts Community.

Translating contemporary art and concepts into writing requires abstract and complex language. This report presents an opportunity for decision-makers to consider who they are speaking to and who is their primary audience. Formal style guides will promote better communication with primary audiences and create a resource for staff to keep writing consistent and on-brand.

Example of academic language in a report:

Although the result of the study was inconclusive, it did confirm the writer's hypothesis and as such, the committee decided to implement the policy.

Example of social writing at a grade 9 level:

The result of the study confirmed the writer's hypothesis. The committee made a new policy.

Recommendations

Specific recommendations will reference W3C Proposed Recommendations from the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) version 2.2 (July 2023), the Accessible British Columbia Act, and other best practice recommendations from Disability resources and communities. Because accessibility laws are still in their infancy in Canada, the ADA and American Accessibility Standards may be recommended at times as a goalpost, aknowleding that SFU Galleries is not currently required to conform to any best practices or accessibility standards per Canadian law. These recommendations seek to make SFU Galleries digital offerings and needs more accessible for users first, while acknowleding that laws, regulations, and policy are being developed currently within SFU that may eventually require SFU Galleries to use and follow.

This report uses for a minimum conformance of Level AA within WCAG which is a foundational level of accessibility. While Level AAA Success Criteria is always a goal worth striving for, there are few developers practicing accessible code such that it conforms to these standards as it often is not a priority for non-Disabled and Hearing centred organizations and clients, and many of the conformance success criterial prioritize functionality and access over design. Design firms themselves may say that they are working to support an accessible web, but unless asked, will not work highter than an A or AA level of conformance unless specifically tasked and budgeted for. As such many softwares and organizations are still using WCAG 2.0 or 2.1 to set their conformance goals, with a minimum level A conformance.

Some feedback will be based on user feedback, and recommendations from the author as an arts worker, audience member, and artist working within the contemporary arts. This holistic approach centres the human experience over design and developer goals. The author of this report prioritizes Disability and Social justice within their recommendations, and follows the 10 principals of Disability Justice as defined by Sins Invalid.

Recommendations that conflict with existing policies, procedures, or guidelines at SFU Galleries, SFU policy and procedures, and recommendations currently in development related to the new Gibson museum planned for 2025 are to be taken into consideration, but the author acknowledges that accessibility is a moving target. Whenever possible, use these recommendations as a starting point to begin a conversation. When a recommendation creates an access conflict for existing or prioritized users, document and consider the risks and opportunities before overwriting existing systems. Creative access is a process and accessibility work is ultimately about people gathering with agency at their own time.

2.1. File naming and shared folders

Navigation tags: SFUGartsWorkers

When a file is needed, a staff member should be able to quickly find out whether a template exists or if they need to create something new. If templates exist, they should be easy to find and identify. File duplication happens because of the need to work around the physical server and due to simple human error; therefore, the administration must schedule file maintenance each year. The initial process will require more labour and time set aside, but once it has been created and practiced, subsequent years will require less time.

Establishing file naming and folder standards helps staff quickly identify duplicate files. It also builds accessibility. When things follow simple naming conventions, it becomes more intuitive for screen reader users, English as additional language users, and those with cognitive challenges to find files themselves or seek assistance.

Note: This recommendation is for files on the shared server. This recommendation may also apply to collections; however, this author acknowledges that library systems and archives may employ their own file naming to work within the existing collections database and at the discretion of the Collections Manager.

2.1.1. File Names: Recommendation and Process

Filenames should be a reasonable length and strive for a maximum of 31 characters. Dates should not be included in the name of files. Should dating be essential, a new folder should be created, and dates should be contained within the content of the file. File names should be lowercase and not use spaces or underscores. Hyphens are recommended.

Example:

Template for communications, event listings on website, version 2023 of the file, word document.

Folder: communications > templates
This is where the file would live,
Folder: communications > templates > Old
This is where versions prior to 2023 would be stored, if they needed to be kept,
File: eventlistings-website.doc
File contents: contains date and year, states that it's a template in the header.

2.1.2. Versions and Nestled Folders: Recommendation and Process

Multiple file versions may be needed to show process or when multiple staff and users are accessing the same file. Files should be named sequentially, and when there is a merged file or final file, this should be identified by the word final. It is also recommended that archived versions of any file be captured in a folder to allow people to quickly detect the most relevant file when navigating the file system.

The best way to incorporate organic file dating is to place a date at the top of every document. Make this a policy for all documents created and used by SFU Galleries.

Example:

Planning budget worksheets for each department at SFU Galleries, Excel spreadsheet.

Folder: Budgets > 2023

Folder: Budgets > 2023 > Planning

File: budget-communications.xls
Working file contained within the Planning folder where communications built their budget. This information might have been copied into the master but is saved seperately as there are notes that are important for planning in 2024, but are not needed by the entire staff. This file is also being used by communications as a working document during the year to project and plan. This file might also live within the Communications department's 2023 planning folder, and has an alias within the Planning folder so that other staff can find it without needing a duplicated file,
File: budget-communications-1-review.xls
A duplicate file made by someone on the communications team who was working from home and dropped it quickly into this folder. They didn't have time to check that the info isn't captured, but wanted to have it on the server. The information might have been copy/pasted into the current master. It will be reviewed and deleted during the coming year's data maintenance day.

During data maintenance days, encourage teams to elminiate duplicates and add dates to document content.

2.1.3. Project File Maintenance Project

This project's goals will be to restore and consolidate data on the shared server, to put into practice a file naming process and folder structure that is intuitive and consistent across SFU Galleries departments, and to create a schedule for regular maintenance to commit to these best practices and create accountability.

It is recommended that file duplication software be purchased. Programs such as Fast Duplicate File Finder Pro identify duplicate and similar files (file names and contents) so that staff can review and confirm that the server is up to date.

In the initial phase, staff will be tasked with finding and restoring all files from offsite locations to the shared server. Following this phase, a duplicate file audit will be run and distributed.

The second phase will schedule time for departments to implement the file naming procedure and update file names and folders. Duplicates will be manually reduced. Once the folder and initial file maintenance concludes, an audit to find similar or duplicate files will confirm that all duplicates have been removed or that similar files are updated with dates and placed into non-active folders with the most recent or active version.

Once this stage is complete, a schedule for annual maintenance will be developed and added to shared calendars for current and new staff. In anticipation of this event annually, staff should be given a deadline to get offsite data back onto servers, and a duplicate file audit can be run.

2.4. Staff Meetings

Navigation tags: SFUGartsWorkers

This chapter is not designed to analyze the current Staff Meeting structure but to suggest ways digital tools and a commitment to hybrid staff meetings can provide more equitable access to all team members. Regardless of who is in the room, having tools that prioritize accessibility and transparency can promote wellness and efficiency. By providing alternative ways that staff can participate and access staff meeting information, SFU Galleries allows its team agency to make decisions based on their daily dynamic needs and reduces the reactive accommodation scramble that may need to happen when a guest or distanced participant needs to attend. Arguably, having systems for staff meetings meant to empower those in the room can also help break up power imbalances (whether acknowledged or unacknowledged) and give those in attendance more authority to speak up and respond when they previously could not. The goal is not to increase the length of staff meetings. Still, it is worth noting that when introducing new procedures into systems that have previously prioritized non-Disabled hearing participation and efficiency, things will slow down as people practice, make mistakes, and are confronted with their own ableism!

The simplest way to practice hybrid staff meetings is to invite Zoom into all staff meetings, regardless of whether they are held in person or online. Schedule a Zoom meeting event for each staff meeting and set up a speakerphone, microphone/laptop for all in-person meetings.

2.4.1. Transcriptions and note taking

A transcription tool is a great addition to all staff meetings. Limited AI programs that generate speech-to-text transcriptions have come a long way in the past five years, with a lot of growth following the early days of the Pandemic when more people used videoconferencing and VOIP. Transcription tools not only serve as a record of the conversation but can also allow for asynchronous participation of offsite or absent staff, as well as provide notes for reference.

Transcription tools are most accurate when one person is speaking at a time. Committing to transcription in meetings encourages people who often struggle to wait for others to complete their thoughts or those with more experience and presence in meetings. Creating meeting procedures that require people to speak one at a time and establishing a verbal or physical indicator that someone is done allows those slower to process to finish their thoughts fully before being interrupted. Modelling this behaviour online, as well as in-person meetings using a conference microphone or mobile devices, can change the dynamic of speakers in a room.

Live transcription also provides auto-captions as an option to staff. Captions can provide access to the hard of hearing and d/Deaf, assist those who better process reading than listening, and help those for whom English is an additional language. By calling into Zoom for staff meetings, this service becomes available to anyone who needs it and has access to SFU Galleries calendars.

For any visuals presented in-person or online, commit to having someone visually describe what is being displayed. If a transcript is running, this can become the basis for description used in other places such as memos, external presentations, or online content.

2.4.2. Agendas and calendar events

Agendas are a handy tool for in-person and online meetings. They can help set goals, establish who should be in the room or have access to asynchronous notes and transcripts, and provide clarity for those attending. Agendas are a similar tool typically printed out for in-person meetings but could be distributed via email or through a shared document.

Agendas can also be included in digital calendar invitations. Prioritizing agendas is a great opportunity to practice making calendar events accessible. Modelling and making time to practice accessible content writing internally makes it easier to do when sending out content externally.

2.5. Manuals and Onboarding

Navigation tags: SFUGartsWorkers

While manuals are important for onboarding, they are also a staff resource for important but irregular tasks. Additionally, manuals can provide an alternative way to access information for various learners. It establishes a best practice and consistent way of doing things to help troubleshoot errors quicker, and it can help workers identify or overcome assumptions and biases. In a manual, best practices can be explained or written out so that staff can focus on consistently providing what is needed for their audiences and customers in most cases, and not need to strategize a solution live every time. Certainly, manuals should be regularly reviewed, critiqued, and renewed. Still, a systems or procedures manual can help keep things consistent, and give staff agency to answer process questions consistent with institutional values, and not subject to the whims of the internet or assumption.

Two manuals were provided to the report's author to review for this needs assessment.

  • SFU Galleries Website Manual
  • SFU Galleries Campaigner (Email/Newsletter) Manual

Both of these manuals have been consulted to generate feedback for Chapter 4 (website) and Chapter 2.8 Email.

This chapter is focused on recommendations for the format of these manuals, how to make these digital assets more accessible, and how to recommend a project to generate or review written documentation for processes that are either known only to current staff or whose formal process documents require an update.

2.5.1. Format

The PDF format is excellent for preserving style and formatting, and when saved correctly, it can provide a small file size that is easy to share. However, PDFs are more difficult to update, and assets such as manuals should be updated, if not annually, regularly to incorporate new knowledge, best practices, and software or hardware changes.

PDFs are also inconsistently accessible for people with screen readers.

It is recommended that any PDF manuals be converted back into a word processor file as the master file. Once updated, a dated PDF version of the manual can be created as an alternate format saved within the same folder. Still, the Word document is available for updates and changes, or if the PDF format is not accessible.

Optional: Just as this report has been written in HTML to prioritize accessibility and allow screen readers to be a priority group, HTML or other markup could be considered for SFU Galleries manuals. A wiki such as MediaWiki could be set up on the local network, which staff could access through the VPN while remaining closed to the public. It would be searchable and follow an accessible format that all SFU Galleries departments could use. Alternatively, since the SFU Galleries staff currently uses Asana, an operations project could be started with the Project Brief linking to manuals within the shared drive. Roles could be defined, and the messages system could be used during the year to note necessary manual updates, leave a request for clarification, or update staff when manuals are changed.

For long training manuals, it can also be true that text is a barrier. Some people are better trained through listening and watching. Consider running manuals through text-to-speech tools such as Speechify to ensure that things are following a natural order and to allow staff, especially neurodiverse folks, alternative ways of learning.

It's also worth running manuals through a readability tool such as the free Hemingway App or pro tools like Readable.com. Manuals should strive to be simple, where understanding is the goal. Use simple language and short sentences. Expand all acronyms. A readability tool can help.

It can be a great project for new staff, short-term workers, interns, and students to review existing manuals for programs they need to become more familiar with. It provides a space for co-learning, where they can develop a new skill, and it can quickly identify places where the manual is unclear or missing information. This has been recommended as a project within the 2.6 Co-learning section.

For people for whom English is an additional language, having manuals available in an accessible, text-based format can also make it easier for language translation tools to be used to help clarify technical or industry-specific language that is not immediately familiar to the new team member.

Consider creating a manual template for all departments that follow the SFU Galleries style guide and make it simple for people to seek out knowledge from other departments in a familiar way.

Add descriptions for visuals and name how they connect to the step-by-step text. Having fewer pages is less important than type size and an easy-to-follow order on a page. Use two columns maximum and consider stacking images with a centre justification and footnote or caption to describe the images' purpose.

2.5.2. Manuals Maintenance Project

Similar to the File Maintenance Project day/week, it is recommended that scheduled time is made during the year for manual updates. In year one, more time would be required to review existing manuals and update them.

In the initial phase, staff will be tasked with identifying and placing all the existing manuals in a shared folder within the server or creating an alias link for department-specific manuals that are better kept within relevant department folders. Consider making this a top-level folder or contained within an onboarding or training folder. Administration and IT would consider whether individual manuals or a browser-based (wiki) intranet would be sustainable.

The second phase will schedule time for departments to prioritize manuals that need an update. New manuals may need to be developed from scratch, which can be added to the project list. Students or interns could be invited to take on a specific project to document drafts during this phase.

Once this stage is complete, a schedule for annual maintenance will be developed and added to shared calendars for current and new staff. This annual maintenance can include updates to existing manuals or scheduling development of new manuals during the coming year..

2.6.3 Asset Description Project

This project is designed to bring in students or project contractors to focus on building descriptions associated with all public facing assets on both the SFU Galleries website and Collections database.

This will be a long-term project, that will span multiple years, and can be renewed annually as new assets are always being generated by SFU Galleries, and as new acquititions are brought into the SFU Art collection.

If more robust funding can be found, this project can also include captions and transcriptions to make audio visual assets more accessible and searchable.

External funding can be found in creative writing, accessibility support, and professional development grants. However, the first phase of this project should be in developing a best practices or style guide that workers can follow to maintain consistency and to achieve SFU Galleries/Collective communication goals.

Best Practices for Descriptions and Translations

The following is a list of recommendations to be added to a style guide or best practices manual for writing descriptions. There are many resources available for purchase and for free online; however, the final guide should be approved by both Collections and Communications teams before the description project begins in earnest.

This guide should be titled Best Practices for Descriptions and Translations to recognize that the purpose of this exercise is both to describe the objects and assets in detail, and that it is a translation of visuals into text. The act of translating visuals into text can be very subjective. The guide is designed to help writers stratle the line between inclusive description (creative access) and functional description (brief, with a focus on keywords). This project will also generate alt tags for online media. This process should be informed by existing media captions; however, media captions and descriptions should have very little in common with each other. They should compliment each other and there should be very little repetition.

If captions and transcriptions are included in this project, style standards should be detailed within this guide.

Descriptions should employ an information hierarchy that starts with the broadest information, and then move into details. This gives readers and listener agency to continue engaging with the description or to move on if it is not relevant.

  • Images: If an image is meant to convey information, a feeling or emotion, or a purpose, follow the best practices for image description from the W3C. If writing about a physical object within the collections, write with enough description such that if the object was lost, it could be described to someone who has never seen or felt it it so that they could identify it.
  • Videos: Describe videos as a synopsis. Media captions for video should contain the duration information. If there are chapters or specific sections of a video that are thematically different, a description should cover each theme. Content warnings can also be contained here. Use high level keywords to help people locate this media. If transcriptions are available, these can be linked within the description or should immediately follow this content.
  • Audio: Describe the audio as a synopsis. For interviews, name the speakers. If there is a transcription available, this should immediate follow the description.
  • Alt tags: What is the purpose or role of this digital asset? An alt tag does not describe content in detail, and when an asset is simply to provide decoration for a sighted viewer, it is essential that the alt tag either be designated as a null attribute (alt="") and by way of the wai-aria attibutes (role="presentation"). If an asset will immediately include a description, alt tags can also be left as a null attribute.
  • Media Captions: A media caption should include a title, creator or artist name, a year, and potentially a line to explain its purpose.

2.9. Working Offsite

Navigation tags: SFUGartsWorkers, SFUGnetwork

SFU Galleries has multiple office locations and scheduled work from home days through VPN. Its staff supports artists through community events, studio and gallery visits, co-learning opportunities and more. SFU Galleries also maintains and activates offsite public artwork. Additionally, staff have some casual flexibility to work away from office locations when health and wellness are a concern.

Working offsite is now so different than it was during a pre-mobile or web era or even prior to 2021. Adjacent to considerations around physical location is the ability to switch bewteen and use mobile devices, either owned by the institution or the staff. Formalized agreeements, proceedure, and inventory associated with working offsite and through mobile devices is something to be considered in future digital strategy and in operations planning. Without a formal proceedure or policy related to working offsite, the burden of planning and coverage is subject to the fluxuating capacity of staff, it puts the burden of managing safety, health and wellness on employees, and it is only sustainable while the current staff is employed and "well". It represents a risk to productivity and capacity as staff cannot rely on a system that is imperminant and subject to the current administrative staff make-up.

Cellphones are now ubiquitous in office culture, and are used by SFU Galleries employees to do their work, including emails. While still being informally considered as extra or up to an employee, they are now common enough to examine how these tools are being used, move beyond assumptions, and document how much cellphone and personal device use is happening and what the cost is. It has been identified by multiple staff that their cellphones are essential for their jobs, from documenting and installation, to artist visits and offsite meetings. In particular, the SFU Collections is stored in a vault where staff are required to go down a set of stairs, often solo, with no cell reception and a single phone that does not dial out.

Without a policy or formalized set of procedures, personal device use poses a potential security risk, requires employees to be self-motivated and update/upgrade their phone for minimum security (os updates, etc), and unjustly benefits employees with more opportunity and priviledge. To assume all employees could afford a cellphone is not advised, but it is worth considering how an employee with access to a cellphone might be more efficient, mobile, reachable, and rewardable when reviewing output.

The author does not recommend SFU Gallery prohibit personal device use from the office, nor to punish or disrupt those who have integrated their phones into the productivity and safety systems, but to conduct deeper investigation into personal device use. It may reveal that mobile or other personal digital devices are in fact essential for work now conducted at SFU Galleries (website and app development, conferencing, file transfer, testing, photography, etc) and should be considered within operations.

A cellphone and personal device use survey of current employees would be a good first step to get a picture of the tech requirements and cost of maintenance that is held by staff, and how cellphones are being used such that it is more convenient to use personal assets than ones provided by SFU Galleries.

Adding cellphone processes to operations manuals and policy helps manage security and expections for all staff, and updating contracts to confirm that cellphone use is the choice of the employee also minimizes risk when it comes to privacy and scams that many small businesses do not consider until they are threatened.

If cellphone use proves to be considerable, it may be recommended that a security audit be conducted and physical phones or personal devices are removed from operations, and that SFU Galleries hardware and internet for all staff are bundled into the same agreement with current internet service providers. Working offsite comes with risks and opportunities to consider outside the scope of a digital assessment, but the practice of working offsite is no longer analog and will only become more possible with the ever growing iot (internet of things).

Continuing on the theme of a siloed approach, the Collections database is an unpolished gem isolated from the content on the SFU Galleries website except through a navigation link. Exhibitions and their assets are not contained with the database, nor are current and upcoming artists, and causes a visitor to wonder if they haven't stumbled into a non-public part of the website. A few content writing projects (with student participation) have been recommended, and there are co-learning opportunities that should be done both with the collections, curation, and communications team so that work is not repeated, and can be used in a variety of places. Ultimately, an important question must be asked and answered by SFU Galleries: What is the scope of the SFU Collections Database, and who does it serve?

It has been recommended, both by this report's author, as well as team members at SFU Galleries, that the CMS system is too robust and clunky for the needs of the public facing website. It is worth considering a move towards a CMS or restructure within the existing CMS that prioritizes exploration and browsing. The integrations within the SFU.ca website presents many challenges and easily confuses a visitor in ways that might benefit SFU but not the galleries, nor the user. SFU Galleries is not SFU, and its website should reflect that. SFU Galleries needs to be focused on a visitor experience, whereas SFU needs to provide a huge wealth of information for a vast variety of users. Making this distinction will help SFU Galleries to better serve their audiences, as a gallery and museum, not as a department of SFU (regardless of how it is on paper).

As SFU Galleries is going through a redesign process right now, the scope of this chapter is restricted to existing pages and content. Design feedback has been kept to a minimum, and the author of this audit is in discussion with the design firm and current staff to provide feedback in the early days of the redesign.

However, a major recommendation is that the website move away from a physical site specific organization of content, and move towards one that is presented chronologically. SFU Galleries should consider more broad and simple categories, with fewer needs to click, with the topmost hierarchy being date, followed by exhibition or special project. Sub taxonomogies would be presented as links and tags that are familiar to a web-user, and would encourage people to explore by a linked interest, rather than requiring those with an interest to go and do more searches or make intuative leaps of logic. For example, if an artist had previously worked with SFU for an event, workshop, exhibition, etc, it is up to the SFU Gallery staff to manually create links to other content that the artist has worked on. Additionally, there are items that are located in the special projects, or publications (sales) areas that are not listed (unless requested of the content manager) and requires the visitor to be self-motivated to search to find related content. The goal should be to provide the end user with options that are intuative and low effort. While the design project is in development, it is recommended that the content team do an information architecture audit to identify content that could be connected and better served to visitors via tags and cross-linking.

It is becomming more and more common for visitors to be able to access content through an infinate scroll (scroll down and more context is listed) or navigation that makes content connections or serves the next relevant content in chronological order. In the current site, it requires someone to navigate back to the side menu, within the current site, and choose from multiple categories (upcoming, upcoming SCA, past, past SCA). SCA means nothing to the author of this report and requires more labour than the average visitor needs to casually browse.

Since a site architecture audit, and move to a new CMS represents a major website project initiative (that might also require independant funding), this report focuses on access and specific content based on the current website; however, the CMS and architecture projects have been added to the action item checklist.

It is recommended for all section landing pages have a short how-to-use paragraph drafted and made avaialble, either via a show/hide link, a seperate page, or accessible modal pop-up. How-to pages can help anyone who is struggling to use the site, and provide insight from the staff who are more familiar with the information and purpose of the page. Here quick visual descriptions can be shared, and if Deaf participation becomes a priority to SFU Galleries, it is also a good place for an ASL video primer to help navigate the site. Specific details on what would be helpful in how-to pages are recommended below.

General feedback:

  • The sfu.ca and sfu.ca/galleries sites have not set the site language making it harder for some access tools to translate, and support users fully.

  • It is recommended that all caps be removed site wide. All-caps is not accessible as it will slow down all readers, especially those with visual or cognitive challenges. Because all words have a uniformed shape, some readers cannot identify words or the shape of letters without increased magnification or voiceover tools. It is debateable whether or not sans serif or serif fonts are more accessible as both have benefits and drawbacks (sans serif being easier for some with reading challenges, and serif being more clear in digital due to unique letter shapes), however, all-caps create barriers for many types of readers, and is not recommended unless it is used for acronyms, or for emphasis in a script (shouting). For readers who have challenges with lower case or sentence case, choosing a font that has unique shapes for similar letters such as b,d,p, and q, is the next best style choice for text.
  • The front page, sfu.ca/galleries is not intuative, and requires users to either guess, be forced to choose an option that doesn't match their reason for visiting, or to scroll down to the footer to see if they can find the information they need. A carousel has been prioritised over the quick menu. It should be noted that carousels are not ideal for all visitors as persons with visual challenges can struggle with rapidly changing images, persons with screen readers may not be able to access the content (especially without image captions or descriptive alt text), and persons with low deterity or who use keyboards to navigate may not be able to easily navigate through the shifting images or a small interface. It has been noted that staff and some test users have observed its easier to navigate the site via an external search engine. This is definitely a need worth addressing.
  • Alt tags are inconsistent through the website. In some cases, the alt tag is not present at all and only the file name is read by a screen reader. This in itself could be a major project to generate relevant alt tags, but could also incorporate a media description project. Alt tags give a simple description of what media or items are, with a goal to inform a user what or why the content is on the page. Role tags can help users, especially those with low-vision or blindness to easily understand the why of media content. Media description can help both sighted and non-sighted visitors understand the context or content of media, which makes the content more accessible, and can provide a reason for users to continue to invest in reading more or investigating related content.

4.3. Accessibility Information

Navigation tags: SFUGartsWorkers, SFUGaudience, SFUGnetwork

SFU Galleries can't be everything to everyone. While universities do need to strive for more universal access due to the large population and cross-section of demographcis they serve, SFU Galleries should focus on their primary audiences and be clear about who they are serving and what their capacity is.

If SFU Galleries are not going to invest in ASL resources, then they don't serve the Deaf community, and that is OK. In fact, a relationship with cultural groups not represented on staff or without considerable work and consideration will always have a higher chance for harm and misunderstanding even when the goal is to be inclusive. Once SFU Galleries knows who they want to serve, consider the needs of these groups. Get it right with these audiences first, and then, when the goal is to build relationships and accessibility for more visitors, address each of these communities with a focus and care that allows for feedback. Building capacity within staff is also essential to serving communities consistently - something that is very important to those who live with barriers and who must adapt constantly to move within a non-Disabled, hearing world.

This isn't to say that SFU Galleries should assume that their current visitor or artist community are non-Disabled, hearing, and without access needs. Instead, consider the cultural groups and communities with whom SFU Galleries has an on-going relationship, and consider those who are struggling or least served by current processes here first.

Don't do things part ways. Don't offer ASL for workshops or talks, if the rest of your site is inaccessible to Deaf communities. Consider co-hosting events with another group that are in relationship with those communities you want to serve, rather than positioning yourself as ready to hold a relationship that you cannot, or do not have capacity to serve.

4.3.1 Access Information By Location:

Draft accessibility information per location. Go for brevity, but have the more detailed information readily available for your public facing staff. The most important information is health and safety, then mobility, and then access.

What barriers exist that would keep someone with health challenges from each location or what is imporant to know?
Allergies (fragrance, soap, air born particles, etc), masking policies and sanitation services, work from home policy, etc.
How would someone be able to quickly find out if access or wellness information changes?
If a mobility device user arrives, will they be able to navigate to all publically accessible locations that someone without a mobility device could?
Are certain "publically" accessible areas temporarily not accessible due to construction?
If there is an "accessible" entrance, is it different than the main location?
Is there an accessible drop off or parking location? How are the sidewalks to this entrance?
Is there sufficent wayfinding tools to get someone to the site?
What landmarks can you provide?
What are the dimensions of the washroom?
Can all bodies use the washroom on site?
If there are accessibility tools or services available for each location, what are they?
Who should people contact with access questions?

If there is one place for an ASL video, it is here as it allows signing community members to know they can request more information in sign. If the team is not ready to engage in sign language requests, do not include a video.

Are there content warnings or considerations for young visitors?
Is the exhibition approporate for youth, school trips, or people dealing with or carrying trauma? Does the show provide support when presenting traumatic content?

4.5. Post Visit

Navigation tags: SFUGartsWorkers, SFUGaudience, SFUGnetwork

Social media is now the repository for most post-event content, especially for visitors. Unfortunately, that makes more work for companies that are interested in following-up on how a visit went or to learn from the observations of their audiences. The burden of keeping track across the wide web often falls to communications teams and their capacity to do more than react to things that are posted, tagged, or linked back in easily detectable ways.

Collecting information on how a visit went can provide insight into how to improve pre-visit information online. However, there is a balance that needs to be struck when requesting, receiving, and using feedback. Not all feedback is equally convertable into action, nor should it be, but the act of receiving feedback is a gift and deserves a place of respect within the digital landscape.

The web allows for anonymous interactivity which can lower the inhibitions of a user. In face to face conversations, it's easier to see the people with whom you are speaking. In an email, although the screen still creates distance, you are directing your feedback to a specific person. In a form, feedback can be hostile or a place for people to leave comments that are emotional and less than constructive. It's also worth noting that people who live with chronic illness, Disability, or who are disabled by society are often frustrated - with few places to get help. Emotions can make it difficult to express constructive feedback in a way that can be understood or used by teams that are inexperienced at moderating and distancing themselves from the content, so care and patience must be practiced. Sometimes, feedback cannot be used. Sometimes feedback is harmful. Sometimes feedback is uncomfortable. All three of these are different, and having a digital risk plan to deal with feedback is a tool that both helps a team make decision quickly in volatile situations, and also makes a plan to support team members who may be subject to harm, intensional or not.

Set aside space within newsletters to ask for feedback if someone has visited a show or performance. Make it easy to find where people can and should provide feedback. The contact page is where people will go to contact the galleries so state that people can provide post-visit feedback here. Newsletters should link back to this page, or invite people to respond directly to the email.

One way to encourage post-visit feedback is with an online survey; however, these passive surveys tend to only be used by a very small visitor population and tend to invite critical or negative feedback, than a place to have a discussion or share positive experiences. Unless the SFU Galleries has a specific request to ask of visitors and subscribers, a generic survey is not recommended. Additionally surveys and forms are difficult to make accessible and create another avenue for spam.

4.7.1. PastPerfect Database Navigation

The PastPerfect Collection Database requires that you know the name of the artist for whom you are searching, or know very specific details about the object. The report's author was only able to find Creator Records, and Object Records (which are the only links between Records).

The external PastPerfect Database can be navigated by search, random images, or an "object" listing. This is ultimately misleading as records are only displayed by Creator Record (artist name). On the first page of Objects, 22 objects across duplicated artists are all called "Untitled" with a record type "Object". It appears that all items have the record type of "object". Should an object not have an image associated with the entry, a generic icon associated with the current SFU branding appears. Of the first 50 entries on this page, 2 have images and both of these images' alt tags are the name of the artist. Due to how inaccessible this page is, and how it does not present the collection in a compelling way, it is recommended that the Objects menu be removed until more work can be done.

The Random Images collection shows object records with duplicate names as all the entries are listed by creator. This navigation requires someone to be sighted and to be ultimately familiar with artists, and is not particularly useful for someone who is searching for similar objects. It is also recommended that this menu be removed.

Since the PastPerfect website is outside of the sfu.ca/galleries website, a seperate contact page should be added to the navigation menu. Within object records, there is a link to send feedback, but for more general inquiries or to inquire about an object that cannot be found, a general contact page is needed.

As the object records only allow the artist as a linked element, it is difficult to present the content beyond the search or as an object list. While many suggestions can be made, based on other museums and galleries whose online collection is available, the PastPerfect system (or available listings) does not appear to be designed for a public audience.

During the community feedback interview with blind artist Tai G., he lamented that he couldn't really use this to browse as he didn't know the name of an artist and was more interested in the objects first. When he tried to find object information, he had to navigate through a list of search results that only showed names, and object titles. It was meaningless. While definitely more frustrating to a non-sighted users who would have no context when browsing the search listings, the results for a sighted individual was also very underwhelming and required the user to be motivated to click through on multiple object records.

PastPerfect does not accept a wide rage of non-English systems or unicode. There is a limited range of "special characters" that can be used, and ultimately is limited in how non-western/non-English names, communities, cultures, and nations can be represented. While PastPerfect remains the system used for collections, and is an asset available to the public, it is recommended that a content page, or paragraph on the about us page be written to recognize this limitation, and for object/people that are forced to have their proper names to anglicized that a notice about this be included within the record.

There is considerable work to be done within the database to catch up to the current collection, and a backlog due to a lack of staff over the past year. It is important to note that any recommendations made within this report will need to be discussed and considered by the Collections Manager as they develop their goals for the database and its role within the gallery. It is recommended that the first goal for SFU Galleries is to determine the purpose and scope of the database. If the PastPerfect database is to be limited to the SFU Arts Collection, specifically objects collected in the vault, and not exhibitions and assets created by SFU Galleries, the database is of limited use to a general visitor audience and its visibility within the website should be reduced. If the database is able to act as an archive, including assets and the history of SFU Galleries, following a major data entry project, the collections could be integrated into the SFU Galleries website as a glossary, archive, and artist director.

4.7.2. PastPerfect Object Record Updates

Searching the PastPerfect database can be extremely frustrating because many object listings lack broad keywords. Currently, the database prioritizes searches that are seeking something in particular. Work can be done to add more content to object entitites to make more entries discoverable within a search, and by linking objects records through fields like Object Name will allow visitors to continue their search adjacently rather than trying to guess at relevant keywords.

Activating a field for Keywords will give collection staff an opportunity to group together records in custom categories that could respond to common queries, popular object types, or even by curatorial theme.

While exhibitions are currently not logged as unique records (or categories) within the database, they are also not represented within object records that have been previously been presented in an exhibition. Even if SFU Galleries does not wish to archive exhibitions and special projects (and their assets) within the collection, a field should be activated within object entries that have been borrowed or used for SFU Galleries exhibitions to inform researcher that historical data beyond the object exists else where on the website. If the collections does move towards archiving exhibitions as entites, these collections could link together objects, and provide a more detailed history to explore.

A major description project has been recommened within the co-learing chapter to update and expand the description field within existing and new object records. For some records, it's adding details for consistency (one listing's medium states "gouache", and another "gouache on paper"). For others, more work can be done specifically within the Description fields to add detail, and to prioritize access. By add more details that include non-sighted audiences, the collection database is more accessible to low-vision or blind audiences, records without images become easier to understand, and more keywords are added to help visitors and researchers find content. As the current collections manager recommends: "I like to write descriptions that allow someone who has never seen the object, should the object be misplaced, to know exactly what they are looking for."

If possible, allow Object Name and Medium to be linked so that users can browse by like objects. The only linked field within an object record is Artist (allowing a visitor to view all records with a similar artist). The proposed Keyword field should also link records with the same keyword.

5.2. Tai G.

Tai (He, Him) is a Blind, partially deaf martial arts instructor, singer, songwriter, therapist, filmmaker and founder of Night Strike Self Defense for the Blind. Tai works with tactile drawings and raised etchings which allow non-visual patrons to feel shapes and designs (both abstract and representational). Instead of employing colour as a means of delineating form, Tai uses various textured lines and blocks to convey depth, materials and elements. The subject matter of the pieces themselves are rather eclectic in nature and range from everyday personal experiences, the natural realm and traditional Chinese folklore.

Self-identity keywords: Blind, Hard of Hearing, English as Additional Language, Immigrant, BIPOC, Tactile, Emerging Artist, Outsider Artist, Musician, Consultant, Community Programmer.

Summary, Tai

Tai: "When I take on projects like this one, I try to put myself in the position of a newly Blind person; someone who is just not very comfortable with using their technology and how that read. For someone like me, I browse the internet daily, and I do a lot of online work so (the website) is usable, but from the perspective of someone who is less techie, less comfortable with finding out different ways to do things...yeah, it needs a lot of a lot more work."

As Tai mentions, he has considerable tech skill and enjoys coming up with ways to find the answers he is looking for. With a stubborness, and desire to push through barriers, Tai is keen to work with institutions to figure out how to include community members who have never even considered that they would be welcome into the conversation.

    7. Conclusion

    This report has been built from a review based on a specific moment in time, designed to identify needs that can be addressed in the immediate future to better serve SFU Gallery staff, artists, and audiences. A new museum will be built in the coming years, and technology will not stand-still. This report is designed to bridge a gap and assist with current programs and procedures, while providing insight into the how and why of digital accessibility so that it can inform future decisions.

    It is the recommendation of this author that two resources be consulted and be brought into the onboarding process for all SFU Galleries staff, but especially those who will be working with the public and online content. These resources are updated regularly, and are primers to help build understanding of the challenges faced by those living with Disabilities and d/Deafness. By always designing digital strategies around those with the most needs, we shift the attention away from the non-Disabled majorities for efficiency, and instead plan in accessibility from the beginning to prioritize inclusion and care.

    W3C: Introduction to Web Accessibility

    Free class on edX.

    This self-paced, accessible workshop provides insights for anyone designing for and using the web! From workshop hosts, and communication and content writers, to tech and design departments, this workshop provides a strong foundation in digital accessibility. Learn why we must make websites and apps work well for people with disabilities, meet international standards, and provide a better user experience for everyone.

    Sins Invalid Disability Justice Primer

    Skin, Tooth, and Bone, The Basis of Movement is Our People, 2nd Edition.

    During the writing of this report, it was specifically mentioned that vocabulary and best practices feel like a constantly shifting target. The author of this report agrees, but reflects that accessibility and vocabulary shift not to other but to include and acknowledge the dynamic nature of the bodies for whom we seek to provide access. As such, the Disibility Justice Primer written by Sins Invalid is a recommended reading resource for all staff and program faciliators to build a foundation for hosting mixed ability programs, as well as concrete access suggestions for events.

    Hardware upgrade

    It is necessary that an upgrade to wifi and cell service be prioritized within the SFU Collections vault. It represents both a safety risk, and does not allow for work to be conducted without travel up and down stairs. It is worth contacting the BC Arts Council and asking about SFU Galleries' eligibility within the Arts Infrastructure Grant, specifically with the category of Capital Improvements.

    Accessibility Committee

    Any working groups with goals towards digital access and needs will inevitably need to be discussing access. While physical access and facility needs are ultimately still seperate from the digital world, it becomes less and less so every year. As the internet becomes more a part of our society and working culture, it becomes more important to position digital accessibility with priority when planning operations, budgets, and programming.

    Following a staff committement to review these assets, it is strongly recommendated that an accessibility committee be formed made up of SFU Galleries staff, and if possible, 2-3 community members associated with the gallery. This could be a local artist, enrolled student, or guest ambassador who are all keen to discuss accessibility as it relates to internal systems, and programming. Consider writing a grant to support the first year of this committee to build capacity for staff, and to apply for accessibility assistance so that accessible meeting practices are modelled by the committee, and allow for Disabled and d/Deaf participation.

    It is important that this committee be familiar with the Social Model of Disability. The social model of disability diverges from the dominant medical model of disability (prioritized by policy within SFU and thus already reflected in HR policy followed by SFU Galleries), where a functional analysis of the body as a machine needs to be fixed in order to conform with normative values. This committee does not need to repeat the work already being done by systems that prioritize effency and ableism, and instead has the opportunity to focus on systemic barriers, and social exclusion which make it impossible for disabled people to be valued fully.

    This committee could gather exclusively online, and would be required to consider accessibility both within the digital and in-person spaces. As digital connectivity becomes ubiquitous with professional work, accessibility work cannot be restricted to physical spaces.

    It is recommended that the first job tackled by the accessibility committee is to create an accessibility statement. This statement would represent the abilities and goals of the SFU Galleries team, and be a first draft in creating something shareable that could live online. Be realistic. What can SFU Galleries do with their current resources, and what do they strive to do? What can they promise, and where can they acknowledge risk or scarcity?

    SFU is engaged in their own accessibility work, especially as the Accessible BC Act goes into effect and all Provincially funded Universities now require accessibility committees and plans. The SFU Galleries accessibility committee would be informed by the conversations happening within contemporary art, as well as the public, and could be a place for co-learning between SFU and the Galleries in coming years.

    Accessibility is not one person's job, and by forming a committee, even an informal one to discuss barriers or challenges in an open forum, it both affirms SFU Galleries committement to accessibility, and provides an opportunity for the work to be shared by people across departments and responsibilies within the gallery.

    Note:

    SFU is currently* implementing interim accomodation procedures (IAP) due to an anticipated incrase in applications for acommodoation of limitations associated with protected characteristics under the Human Rights Code, other than physical or mental disability, in the workplace. "For some time, the University has had a policy – GP40 (Disability Accommodations in the Workplace) – to address applications for the accommodation of limitations associated with a physical or mental disability. However, Policy GP40 and its associated process, which is managed by Wellness & Recovery, does not address applications for the accommodation of a limitation associated with the Protected Characteristics. These requests were typically addressed directly between an employee and the employee’s supervisor or manager with, in some cases, the involvement of Human Resources or Faculty Relations and the employee’s Union or APSA. The IAPs are designed to replace this informal approach with a timely, effective, consistent, and transparent process, especially at a time when the University is anticipating an increase in Applications."

    As such, current SFU policy requires that they determine the needs of the SFU staff member (medical model of Disability), and does not allow for self-identification of the individual. SFU may hire their own professionals to assess the employee's physical or mental capacity.

    This information may be out of date. This procedure was meant to be implemented on an interim basis because the University was revising Policy GP40 so that it addresses the accommodation of limitations associated with the Protected Characteristics. Once the revision of Policy GP40 has been completed the revised Policy GP40 will replace the IAPs. It was estimated that this would be completed by 2021, but the procedure remains active on the website.
